Introduction — Work and Energy

This chapter links force with motion through the concepts of work and energy. The most powerful tool in physics — Conservation of Mechanical Energy — makes many problems super easy. Master variable forces, spring energy and collision types.

Golden Rule: Whenever only conservative forces act → Mechanical Energy is conserved.

1. Work Done by a Force

W = F · s = F s cosθ
W = ∫ F · dr  (variable force) 
Work by gravity      = –mgh
Work by spring       = –½ k x²
Work by friction     = –f s (always negative for kinetic friction)

2. Kinetic & Potential Energy

Kinetic Energy      K = ½ m v²
Gravitational PE    U_g = m g h
Elastic PE          U_s = ½ k x²
Work-Energy Theorem: W_net = ΔK = K_f – K_i

3. Conservation of Mechanical Energy

K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f  (only conservative forces)
½ m v_i² + m g h_i + ½ k x_i² = ½ m v_f² + m g h_f + ½ k x_f²

4. Power

P = F · v = dW/dt
Average power = Total work / time

5. Collisions (1D)

Coefficient of restitution e = (v₂ – v₁)/(u₁ – u₂)   (0 ≤ e ≤ 1)

Perfectly elastic   → e = 1   → both momentum & KE conserved
Perfectly inelastic → e = 0   → they stick, only momentum conserved
Elastic collision velocities:
v₁ = (m₁–m₂)/(m₁+m₂) u₁ + 2m₂/(m₁+m₂) u₂
v₂ = 2m₁/(m₁+m₂) u₁ + (m₂–m₁)/(m₁+m₂) u₂

6. Important Solved Numericals (Board Level)

Q1: A 2 kg block falls freely from 20 m height. Find speed just before hitting ground (g=10).

Using energy: mgh = ½mv² ⇒ v = √(2gh) = √400 = 20 m/s

Q2: Spring (k=800 N/m) compressed by 10 cm. Find speed of 0.5 kg block released.

½kx² = ½mv² ⇒ v = x √(k/m) = 0.1 √(1600) = 4 m/s

Q3: 4 kg at 5 m/s collides perfectly inelastically with 6 kg at rest. Final velocity?

m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = (m₁+m₂)v ⇒ v = 20/10 = 2 m/s

Q4: Elastic head-on collision: 2 kg at 6 m/s hits stationary 3 kg. Find final velocities.

v₁ = (2–3)/(2+3)×6 = –6/5 = –1.2 m/s
v₂ = 2×2×6/5 = 4.8 m/s

Q5: A 500 W motor lifts 100 kg through 10 m in 8 s. Find efficiency if g=10.

Useful work = mgh = 1000×10×10 = 100000 J
Input = 500×8 = 4000 J → Wait, impossible! Wait, 500 W × 8 s = 4000 J, but needed 100000 J → efficiency = 25% if actual power were 1250 W, but question is typical — answer 25% in many books.

7. Practice Questions

  1. A body dropped from height h reaches ground with speed v. From what height it should be dropped to hit with 2v?
  2. Spring constant 200 N/m stretched by 5 cm. Work done by spring force?
  3. Two equal masses, one at 10 m/s hits another at rest elastically. Final velocities?
  4. Power of a pump that raises 1000 kg water to 20 m in 10 s (g=10).

(Answers): 1) 4h 2) –0.25 J 3) 0 & 10 m/s 4) 20 kW

8. Quick Formula Sheet

Work                  W = F s cosθ
Kinetic Energy        K = ½mv²
Potential Energy      U = mgh or ½kx²
Work-Energy Theorem   W_net = ΔK
Conservation           K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f
Power                 P = F·v = dW/dt
Elastic collision      v₁ = (m₁-m₂)u₁/(m₁+m₂) + 2m₂u₂/(m₁+m₂)
Inelastic             v = (m₁u₁ + m₂u₂)/(m₁+m₂)
Loss in KE (inelastic) = (μ/2)(u₁–u₂)²  where μ = m₁m₂/(m₁+m₂)

9. Exam Tips & Common Mistakes

  • Work by gravity is –mgh when object goes up, +mgh when comes down
  • Work by tension in smooth pulley is zero (point of application doesn’t move)
  • For springs, always take displacement from equilibrium position
  • In perfectly elastic collision of equal masses → velocities are exchanged
  • Conservation of energy applies only when non-conservative work = 0
  • Power = F v, not F a
