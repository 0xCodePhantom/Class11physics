Introduction — What is Measurement?

Measurement is the language of physics. We describe physical quantities by assigning numbers to them using agreed standards (units) and methods. This chapter builds the foundations: types of quantities, units, dimensions, errors and significant figures — all the practical tools you need to make, scale and reason about measurements.

Why this matters: Every physical law uses consistent units. Mistakes in units are the most common source of errors in experiments and numerical problems. Learn to convert, estimate, and track dimensions early — it saves time and avoids silly mistakes later.

This file has been structured to be both readable and actionable: conceptual explanations, worked examples, practice questions, and quick-reference tables. Use the table of contents in the sidebar to jump to any part of the chapter.

1. Physical Quantities: Base & Derived

Physical quantities describe measurable aspects of the physical world. They fall into two broad categories:

  1. Base quantities: chosen by convention (e.g., length, mass, time, electric current, thermodynamic temperature, amount of substance, luminous intensity). In SI, there are seven base quantities.
  2. Derived quantities: combinations of base quantities (e.g., speed = length/time, force = mass × acceleration).
Memorize the seven SI base quantities and their units — they're used everywhere. A quick mnemonic: Length, Mass, Time, Current, Temperature, Amount, Light.

SI base quantities and units (concise table)

Length (L) — metre (m)
Mass (M) — kilogram (kg)
Time (T) — second (s)
Electric current (I) — ampere (A)
Temperature (Θ) — kelvin (K)
Amount of substance (N) — mole (mol)
Luminous intensity (J) — candela (cd)

Notation: In dimensional analysis we use L, M, T, I, Θ, N, J (or sometimes simply L,M,T...). When writing dimensions we use square brackets, e.g., [velocity] = [L T-1].

Example: Area = length × length → [Area] = L2

2. Units, Standards and SI Prefixes

Units are standardized references for measurement. SI (Système International) is the modern standard system used in science. Important: prefixes change the scale of units. Learn the common prefixes and their powers of ten.

Common SI prefixes

kilo (k)  = 103
centi (c) = 10-2
milli (m) = 10-3
micro (µ)  = 10-6
nano (n)   = 10-9
mega (M)   = 106
Giga (G)   = 109

Tip: Instead of memorizing too many prefixes, practice converting everyday values — grams, metres, milliseconds. Intuition grows fast when you use magnitudes in examples.

Unit conversion — worked example

Q: Convert 5.4 km to metres.
Solution

1 km = 1000 m, so 5.4 km = 5.4 × 1000 = 5400 m.

Q: Convert 1200 ms to seconds.
Solution

1000 ms = 1 s, so 1200 ms = 1.2 s.

3. Dimensional Analysis

Dimensional analysis is a powerful method to check equations for consistency, deduce relationships, and convert between units. Always check the dimensions of both sides of an equation.

Rules

  • Only quantities with the same dimensions can be added or subtracted.
  • Dimensions must match on both sides of a physically valid equation.
  • Use dimensions to find the form of an equation when the constants are unknown (up to dimensionless numbers).
Example: Check if s = ut + 1/2 at2 is dimensionally consistent.

Left side: [s] = L. Right side: [ut] = [L T-1 × T] = L; [at2] = [L T-2 × T2] = L. Both terms are length — consistent.

Common dimensional formulas

Velocity: [v] = L T^-1
Acceleration: [a] = L T^-2
Force: [F] = M L T^-2
Energy: [E] = M L^2 T^-2
Pressure: [P] = M L^-1 T^-2

Dimensional analysis cannot determine dimensionless constants (like 2, π, e) nor the exact numerical factors; it only gives the form of relations.

4. Errors, Uncertainty & Significant Figures

No measurement is perfect. Understanding and reporting uncertainty is crucial.

Types of errors

  • Systematic errors: reproducible biases (e.g., zero error, calibration error). They shift measurements consistently.
  • Random errors: statistical fluctuations due to measurement limitations (e.g., reading a needle scale).
  • Gross errors: mistakes/accidents (recording wrong value) — ideally avoidable.

Absolute & Relative error

Absolute error: the uncertainty in the units of the quantity, Δx.

Relative error: Δx/x — often expressed as a percentage: (Δx/x) × 100%.

Q: A length is measured as 2.30 m ± 0.02 m. Calculate relative error.

Relative error = 0.02/2.30 ≈ 0.008695 → 0.8695%.

Propagation of errors (basic rules)

When combining measured quantities, use approximate propagation formulas:

  • Addition/Subtraction: Absolute errors add. If z = x ± y, Δz ≈ Δx + Δy.
  • Multiplication/Division: Relative errors add. If z = x × y or z = x/y, (Δz/z) ≈ (Δx/x) + (Δy/y).
  • Power: If z = xn, (Δz/z) ≈ |n| (Δx/x).
These are first-order approximations (valid when errors are small). For more accuracy use partial derivatives (general method): Δf ≈ Σ |(∂f/∂xi)| Δxi.

5. Significant Figures & Rounding

Significant figures show the precision of a measurement. Rules:

  • Non-zero digits are always significant.
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant.
  • Leading zeros are not significant.
  • Trailing zeros are significant if there is a decimal point.

Q: How many significant figures are in 0.00320?

Answer: 3 significant figures (3, 2, and trailing 0 after the decimal).

When performing calculations, keep one extra guard digit during intermediate steps and round the final answer to the correct number of significant figures.

6. Standards, Calibration & Instruments

Precision depends on the instrument. Always choose an instrument with resolution fine enough for your measurement. Common instruments and their typical resolutions:

  • Meter scale (tape) — mm to cm
  • Vernier caliper — 0.01 cm (~0.1 mm) typical
  • Micrometer — 0.001 cm (~0.01 mm)
  • Stopwatch — 0.01 s typical (digital)

Zero error

If an instrument reads a non-zero value when it should read zero (e.g., vernier caliper jaws closed), correct your measurement by subtracting the zero error.

Q: Vernier reads 2.35 cm but zero error is +0.02 cm. True value?

Corrected value = 2.35 - 0.02 = 2.33 cm.

7. Worked Examples (Detailed)

Example 1: A car travels 360 km in 4 hours. What is its average speed in m/s?

360 km = 360,000 m. 4 hours = 14,400 s. Speed = 360,000 / 14,400 = 25 m/s.

Example 2: Dimensional analysis to find the period T of a simple pendulum depends on length l and gravitational acceleration g. Use dimensions to show T ∝ sqrt(l/g).

Assume T = k la gb. Dimensions: [T] = T, [l] = L, [g] = L T-2. Equate: T = La (L T-2)b = La+b T-2b. So exponents: for T: 1 = -2b ⇒ b = -1/2. For L: 0 = a + b ⇒ a = -b = 1/2. So T ∝ l1/2 g-1/2 ⇒ T ∝ sqrt(l/g).

Example 3 (Error propagation): Area of rectangle A = l × w. Measurements: l = 12.0 ± 0.1 cm; w = 8.0 ± 0.1 cm. Find A and its uncertainty.

A = 96.0 cm2. Relative errors: (Δl/l)=0.1/12.0 = 0.00833; (Δw/w)=0.1/8 = 0.0125. Relative error in A ≈ 0.00833 + 0.0125 = 0.02083 ⇒ ΔA ≈ 96.0 × 0.02083 ≈ 2.00 cm2. So A = 96.0 ± 2.0 cm2.

Example 4 (Unit conversion trick): Convert 72 km/h to m/s quickly.

72 km/h = 72 × (1000 m)/(3600 s) = 72 × (1/3.6) ≈ 20 m/s.

More worked examples are helpful — try to replicate each method, then apply to slightly altered inputs to test mastery.

10. FAQs & Study Tips

  • How to remember SI prefixes? Group them in thousands: (k, M, G) and sub-thousand (m, µ, n). Practice with real numbers like 0.001 m, 0.000001 m.
  • Should I memorize dimensional formulas? Memorize a few (force, energy, pressure) and derive the rest by combining base dimensions.
  • Exam tip: Always include units in answers and keep at least two significant figures unless asked otherwise.
